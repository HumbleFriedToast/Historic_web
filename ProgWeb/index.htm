ROBERT OPPENHEIMER

J. Robert Oppenheimer, born on April 22, 1904, was an American theoretical physicist and professor of physics at the University of California, Berkeley. He is best known for his role as the scientific director of the Manhattan Project, the World War II undertaking that developed the first nuclear weapons. Oppenheimer's leadership and contributions to the project earned him the title of "father of the atomic bomb."

Oppenheimer's early academic career was marked by exceptional achievements, including his groundbreaking work in quantum mechanics and theoretical physics. He made significant contributions to the understanding of neutron stars, black holes, and quantum field theory.

After the war, Oppenheimer became deeply involved in promoting international control of nuclear power and advocating for civilian control over nuclear energy. However, his political views and associations led to suspicions during the McCarthy era, resulting in the revocation of his security clearance in 1954.

Despite facing personal and professional challenges, Oppenheimer continued his research and teaching, leaving a lasting legacy in the fields of physics and nuclear science. He died on February 18, 1967, leaving behind a complex and enduring legacy as one of the most influential scientists of the 20th century.
